Overview

02 ​​Poisson Blending

Here we come, to image blending! 

Say we want to blend some part of a source image s a.k.a. the source region S onto a target image t to form a new image v. Let's first talk about the part of preprocessing work we have to do before blending, which is to select the source region and align it with the target image. In my experiment, I set the source region to be an arbitrary polygon inside the source image, selecting vertices of the polygon in counterclockwise order and then generating an 0-1 mask of the source region. Then to align it with the target image, I first rescale it and then translate it to where in the target image it should be blended at. Now we have the aligned source image, the target image and the mask indicating the source region. We could start poisson blending!

Using the same idea as in part1, we first compute the x and y gradients from s inside the source region, then use all the gradients, plus pixel intensities of t on the border of S, to solve for the part of v inside the area of S. We can formulate our objective as follows, which could also be solved as a least squares problem. The rest of v outside the area of S is just the same as the target image t.

FINAL PROJECT 2

Lightfield Camera

Overview

You may notice that when taking everyday photos, things that you are not focusing on may blur. Once you snap it, there's no way to recover a sharper image of what has been blurred in the photo. This means that you must decide what you want in focus before you take the photo. Or do you? Think about how cool it would be if you can simulate countles post-processing effects changing focus, adjusting aperture as you want even after your photo was taken. That's exactly what a lightfield camara enables you. 

In this project, we will ​use the lightfield data from the Stanford Light Field Archive and focus on the two post-processing effects mentioned just above: depth refocusing and aperture adjustment. Going through this project gives me a deep understanding of the lightfield and how we could generate different kinds of photos through it, which is really rewarding. 

01 Depth Refocusing

Change your focus as you want in a photo taken by lightfield camara!

Each photo in the Stanford dataset is represented as a collection of 289 sub-aperture images taken over a 17 x 17 grid on a plane orthogonal to the optical axis. You can think of each sub-aperture image as the light that comes through different part of some universal aperture. Say that part is a small area around (u,v) on the universal aperture. Then the whole bunch of images could be seen as a light field, L(u,v,s,t), which is the light travelling through (u,v) on the aperture plane and (s,t) on the image plane. ​As Ng. et al. describe in this paper, a photo E generated through light field L is equal to the following, where alpha is a measure of the focal length. ​"Examining this equation reveals the important observation that re-focusing is conceptually just a summation of shifted versions of these sub-aperture images." For each image, that shift can be determined by the image's aperture coordinates u,v.  

In my depth refocusing algorithm, I chose the sub-aperture image corresponding to grid point (8,8) as my center image and shifted all other images towards it. For each image, the shift amount is ​the difference of that image's ​u,v position and the center image's ​u,v position scaled by a factor C. After shifting, I simply averaged all the shifted images to produce my result. The smaller C is, the more distant objects would be focused on.

02 ​​Aperture Adjustment

Change the aperture size for a overall-sharp or focused photo!

Based on our understanding of the light field, aperture adjustment with lightfield data is rather simple. As mentioned above, each sub-aperture image can be thought of as the light that comes through u,v on the aperture plane. Let the u,v coordinates of the image corresponding to grid point (8,8) as our aperture center. Then, to set the aperture radius to r is just to only include those images with u,v ​inside the circle of radius r to the aperture center when taking the average.
